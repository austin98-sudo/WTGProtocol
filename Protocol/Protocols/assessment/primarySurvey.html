1. Form general impression of the patient. Consider apperance, work of breathing, and circulation to skin. If a life-threatening condition is found, treat it immediately. · · · · · ·
2. Assess patient's mental status (maintain spinal immobilization if needed).
a. Alert | Responds to Verbal stimuli | Responds to Painful stimuli | Unresponsive to verbal/painful stimuli (no gag or cough). · · · · · ·
b. If the victim is unresponsive with no breathing or no normal breathing (i.e. only gasping), see CARDIAC ARREST - ADULT or CARDIAC ARREST - PEDIATRIC. · · · · · ·
3. Address any MASSIVE HEMORRHAGE
a. Locate the source of any major bleeding · · · · · ·
b. Provide direct pressure until a clot forms, perferably with hemostatic gauze in a wound packing technique · · · · · ·
c. If necessary, utilize commercial tourniquets to control bleeding, DO NOT DELAY TOURNIQUET APPLICATION - IF IN DOUBT, APPLY TOURNIQUET · · · · · ·
4. Assess the patient's AIRWAY. Provide manual in-line stabilization of the head and neck for suspected spinal injury
a. Use the sniffing position or the jaw thrust maneuver (suspected trauma) and to open the airway. Note: Do not hyperextend the neck in infants and small children. · · · · · ·
b. Suction the airway as necessary · · · · · ·
c. Consider maintenance of the airway with an oropharyngeal or nasopharyngeal airway as necessary. · · · · · ·
d. For a complete airway obstruction, see the FOREIGN BODY AIRWAY OBSTRUCTION protocol. · · · · · ·
5. Assess the patient's RESPIRATORY STATUS
a. If respirations are inadequate, assist breathing by giving 1 breath every 6 seconds · · · · · ·
b. If respirations are adequate, consider administering oxygen as needed per provider assessment. · · · · · ·
6. Assess the patient's CIRCULATION
a. Assess the pulses at appropriate pulse points · · · · · ·
b. Reassess for additional bleeding and control any found · · · · · ·
c. Check perfusion by evaluating skin color, temperature, and moisture · · · · · ·
7. Address HYPOTHERMIA as applicable · · · · · ·
8. Assess DISABILITY using the GLASGOW COMA SCALE · · · · · ·
9. EXPOSE the patient. Remove the clothing from pertinent ares of the patient's body for examination · · · · · ·
10. Determine the patient disposition. Expedite transport for high priority patients. · · · · · ·

Key Points - Primary Survey

  • It should be remembered that while the steps in the protocol above are written in a specific order, providers should accomplish most tasks simultaneously utilizing available personnel on scene. A team approach to patient assessment with lifesaving intervention is ideal.
  • Consider neck injury while evaluating the airway in the following patients:
    • All deceleration injuries (motor vehicle crashes, falls)
    • All trauma vicitims who complain of neck pain
    • All trauma victims with neck tenderness or deformity
    • All non-alert patients with possible trauma
  • In drowning, if ther is no spontaneous breathing, remove the patient from the water immediately and begin resuscitation. Minimize in-water resuscitation
  • While evaulating breathing and circulation, expose the thorax, assess for and manage life-threatening signs and symptoms:
    • Assure bilateral breath sounds
    • Assess for jugular vein distension
    • Assess for tracheal deviation
    • Seal sucking wounds with an occlusive dressing
    • Splint flail segments by using bag-mask ventilation
    • For tension pneumothorax, follow THORACOSTOMY, NEEDLE or THORACOSTOMY, FINGER [APM] protocol
  • Pulse oximetry should be used as an adjunct assessment tool and should be recognized that there are situations in which the reading given may no be accurate (i.e., massive hemorrhage, anemia, carbon monoxide poisoning, etc.)
